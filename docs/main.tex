% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a granular, function-by-function analysis of Spatial Entropy Differential Evolution (SEDE). Unlike aggregate studies, we dissect the performance behaviors across distinct dimensional tiers: 30D, 50D, and 100D. By analyzing specific landscape topologies—from the convex slopes of Sphere to the treacherous valleys of Rosenbrock—we demonstrate SEDE's ability to autonomously regulate exploitation and exploration using Shannon Entropy, effectively mitigating the "Curse of Dimensionality."
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Evolutionary Computing, Large Scale Global Optimization, Entropy, Metaheuristics, Swarm Intelligence.
\end{IEEEkeywords}

% SECTION I: INTRODUCTION

\section{Introduction}
Global optimization in high-dimensional spaces remains a persistent challenge in computational intelligence \cite{b1}. As dimensionality increases, the volume of the search space expands exponentially, causing standard metaheuristics like Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) \cite{b2} and Genetic Algorithms (GA) \cite{b3} to suffer from premature convergence or stagnation.

Recent approaches in Large Scale Global Optimization (LSGO) have focused on cooperative co-evolution \cite{b4} and adaptive parameter control \cite{b5}. However, these methods often introduce complex hyperparameters that require manual tuning. In this study, we propose Spatial Entropy Differential Evolution (SEDE), a parameter-free framework that utilizes the thermodynamic concept of entropy to dynamically scale search step sizes. We validate SEDE against established baselines across 67 benchmark instances ranging from 30 to 100 dimensions.

\section{Proposed Method: SEDE}
The core hypothesis of SEDE is that the diversity of a population can be quantified as "information," and this information can drive the search strategy.

\subsection{Entropy as a Diversity Metric}
We define the population entropy $H(g)$ at generation $g$ using Shannon's Information Theory \cite{b6}:
\begin{equation}
H(g) = - \sum_{i=1}^{N} p_i \log p_i
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the population size and $p_i$ is the fitness probability of individual $i$, normalized across the current population. High entropy indicates a scattered population (Exploration phase), while low entropy indicates clustering (Exploitation phase).

\subsection{Adaptive Mutation Scaling}
Standard Differential Evolution (DE) \cite{b7} relies on a fixed scaling factor $F$. SEDE replaces this with a dynamic $F_{new}$, derived from the real-time entropy state:
\begin{equation}
F_{new} = F_{min} + (F_{max} - F_{min}) \times \frac{H(g)}{H_{max}}
\end{equation}
This feedback loop allows the algorithm to "breathe": expanding search steps when diversity is high and contracting them as the population converges, without human intervention.

\section{Experimental Setup}
To ensure a fair comparison, all algorithms utilized identical computational budgets:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Population Size:} $N=50$
    \item \textbf{Max Iterations:} 1000
    \item \textbf{Dimensions:} 30D, 50D, 100D
    \item \textbf{Benchmarks:} CEC-style functions (Unimodal, Multimodal, Composition).
    \item \textbf{Baselines:} DE \cite{b7}, PSO \cite{b2}, GA \cite{b3}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
This granular analysis confirms that SEDE's advantage is not limited to simple functions. By systematically testing across 30, 50, and 100 dimensions, we observe that the Spatial Entropy mechanism provides a distinct "scalability shield," preventing the exponential degradation of performance seen in classical Swarm Intelligence methods.
